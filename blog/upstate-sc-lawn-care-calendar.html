Upstate SC Lawn Care Calendar: Month-by-Month Guide for Healthy Grass

Published June 21, 2026  |  Redwood Landscaping LLC  |  Serving Slater-Marietta, Greenville & Upstate SC

If you live in Slater, Marietta, Greenville, or anywhere in Upstate South Carolina, you know our soil isn't like the rest of the country. Our red clay soil—technically Cecil sandy clay loam—holds nutrients well but compacts like concrete and drains poorly. Pair that with hot, humid summers and mild winters, and lawn care here is its own beast.

This calendar walks you through exactly what to do each month to keep your lawn thick, green, and weed-free — whether you have fescue, bermuda, or a mixed Upstate lawn.

Understanding Upstate SC Grass Types

Most lawns in the Upstate are tall fescue (cool-season, stays green year-round, needs shade) or bermuda (warm-season, loves full sun, goes dormant brown in winter). Many yards have both — fescue in the shade, bermuda in the sun. Your care schedule depends on what you've got.

Monthly Lawn Care Calendar

🌱 Spring (March–May)

March

Fescue: Apply pre-emergent (prodiamine) when soil hits 55°F — usually mid-March in Slater-Marietta. Don't seed yet. Bermuda: Scalp mow to 1" to remove dead top growth. Soil test if you haven't in 2 years.

April

Fescue: Light fertilization (1 lb N/1000 sq ft). Start mowing at 3.5". Bermuda: Fertilize when 50% green-up (typically mid-April). Mow at 1.5" every 5-7 days. Watch for spring dead spot.

May

Ramp up mowing frequency. Fescue: Mow tall (4") to shade out summer weeds. Bermuda: Peak growth — mow twice weekly. Apply grub control if you've had issues. Edge beds and start irrigation schedule.

☀️ Summer (June–August)

June

Critical month for fescue. Raise mower to 4". Water deep and infrequent (1" per week in one shot). Bermuda: Fertilize again. Watch for armyworms and chinch bugs. Brown patch fungus appears in fescue now — don't water at night.

July

Survival mode for fescue. Do NOT fertilize — it pushes tender growth that burns. Water 1-1.5" weekly. Bermuda: full sun = full speed. Mow every 4-5 days. Spot-treat weeds (crabgrass is peaking).

August

Hottest month. Fescue may thin — that's normal. Plan fall overseeding now (order seed). Bermuda: keep mowing, watch for drought stress. Start reducing irrigation on bermuda as days shorten. Good month for hardscaping projects.

🍂 Fall (September–November)

September

THE most important month for Upstate lawns. Fescue: core aerate, then overseed with a quality fescue blend (not big-box seed). Apply starter fertilizer. Water lightly 2-3x daily for germination. Bermuda: last fertilizer application.

October

Fescue: second fertilization — this is the most important feeding of the year. Keep watering new seed. Mow at 3" once established. Bermuda: reduce mowing frequency. Apply winterizer (high K) fertilizer. Core aeration helps red clay drainage.

November

Final mow for fescue at 3". Rake and remove heavy leaf cover — smothering kills grass over winter. Bermuda goes dormant. Last fertilization for fescue (winterizer). Blow out irrigation lines before first freeze.

❄️ Winter (December–February)

December

Dormant season for both grasses. Keep leaves off lawn. Winter weeds (henbit, chickweed) show up — pull or spot-treat on mild days. Good time to service equipment and plan spring projects.

January–February

Quiet months. Walk your property on mild days and pull winter weeds. Apply pre-emergent in late February if soil temps are warming early (use a soil thermometer — target 55°F at 2" depth). Plan landscape design changes now.

Red Clay Soil Tips

Our Upstate red clay needs special handling:

  • Aerate every year. Red clay compacts into brick. Core aeration opens it up for water, air, and nutrients. September is ideal for fescue lawns.
  • Don't overwater. Clay holds water. 1" per week is plenty. More than that and you're inviting fungus and root rot.
  • Add organic matter when you can. Top-dressing with compost (¼" layer) after aeration helps break up clay over time.
  • Lime as needed. Upstate clay tends acidic (pH 5.0–5.8). Most lawns need lime every 2–3 years. Soil test first.
  • Mow high on fescue. Taller grass = deeper roots = better drought tolerance in clay.
